The Real Cost of AI: An Infrastructure Arms Race

AI is not just a software update; it is a massive infrastructure project that requires immense capital.

Microsoft spent $37.5 billion on capital expenditures in its most recent quarter, up 66% year over year, with most of it funding servers and chips. Its free cash flow fell as spending rose. Meta projected $162â$169 billion of total expenses in 2025, driven by infrastructure costs and the talent to run it.

This spending can create durable businesses. It can also create low-return assets and intense margin pressure. Excitement about technology doesn't pay dividends. Cash flow exceeding expectations does.

Revenue Growth Has a Price

The recent revenue growth is undeniable:

  • Microsoft: $81.3 billion revenue, 17% growth
  • Meta: $59.8 billion revenue, 24% growth

Those are real numbers. But they come at a cost.

When capital spending rises this quickly, shareholders fund the buildout first. Profits may or may not follow. Even when new technologies succeed, competition eventually pushes prices down and returns settle closer to average. This is the core risk of an arms race: each firm spends to avoid falling behind, not because the returns on that capital are certain.

Concentration Risk Inside Your "Diversified" Fund

Many investors still see the S&P 500 as 500 independent companies. It has become a top-heavy index whose performance swings on the fortunes of a few mega-cap technology firms.

This concentration happened through drift, not by design. As tech stocks outperformed, market-cap weighted index funds mechanically bought more of them. Portfolios that started balanced became tech-heavy without a single active decision.

This AI buildout adds a second layer of concentration, as many of the same top companies make the same capital-intensive bet at the same time. Owning the market gives you exposure. The danger is owning far more than you realize through overlapping, concentrated funds.

Deconstructing the $625 Billion Backlog

The term "backlog" implies certainty. The reality is more complex.

Backlog is a mix of contracted commitments, revenue timing assumptions, and customer credit risk. Microsoft expects its backlog to convert to revenue over an average of 2.5 years. Again, 45% of that total relies on a single counterparty.

This raises questions investors cannot answer from the outside:

  • How stable is the customer's funding and long-term demand?
  • What happens if the customer renegotiates contract terms?
  • How will new competition affect future pricing power?

While backlog is a meaningful indicator, it is not cash in the bank.

A Historical Parallel: The Dot-Com Buildout

In the late 1990s, companies poured billions into internet infrastructure. The technology fundamentally changed the economy. Yet investors in those companies suffered deep, prolonged drawdowns because valuations ran far ahead of realized profits.

Being right about a technology's direction does not guarantee a positive return from owning its stock at today's prices.

We are not forecasting a market crash. We are highlighting the structure of the risk: high expectations, high spending, and tight competition create a narrow path to success for investors.
